"Contingent Congress: Shared Issue Attention in the U.S. House of Representatives, 1873-2010."
Abstract: Does the U.S. Congress retain a communicative capacity beneath its observed partisan polarization? Current scholarship largely answers in the negative, depicting Congress as an institution paralyzed by polarization, where a member's speech primarily serves individual or partisan branding. This account suggests little room for members' communicative acts. Drawing on existing work on congressional networks, we add an analytically distinct layer---a discourse network---that captures the endogenous communicative activity through which members jointly generate the shared reality of the legislature. To operationalize this layer, we introduce the concept of Shared Issue Attention (SIA)---the alignment of public signals weighted by topical relevance---measured using House members' congressional speeches in the 43rd--111th Congresses (1873--2010). Our analysis includes two complementary parts: a structural analysis of the topology of the congressional SIA network, and a causal analysis of speech diffusion as the mechanism constructing SIA. Structurally, the network exhibits small-world properties and a dominant share of cross-partisan triads. Member-to-member speech diffusion, identified via double negative control designs, is pervasive, cross-cutting, and reciprocal: inter-party diffusion tracks intra-party diffusion in magnitude, and minority-to-majority influence is persistent across the 138 years of congressional history. Together, these findings portray Congress as a communicatively self-organizing system whose dynamic stability is actively reproduced through the members' own deliberative activity.

"Informal Mediation: Shared Issue Attention and Bipartisan Coordination in the U.S. House of Representatives, 1873-2010."
Abstract: For congressional mediation to function effectively, members must engage in social influence processes that can facilitate behavioral coordination, such as roll call cooperation. We argue that members participate in these informal coordination processes through making public speeches on the congressional floor. Analyzing policy speeches in the House from the 43rd to 111th Congresses, we demonstrate that issue attention serves as an informal mechanism for promoting bipartisan coordination in legislative voting. Our analysis documents evidence contrary to pervasive skeptical views regarding congressional rhetoric. First, by measuring and modeling Shared Issue Attention (SIA), we confirm that SIA cannot be reduced to mere partisan rhetoric. Second, we find that SIA exerts a significant positive effect on legislative voting agreement (4.1 percentage points per 1 standard deviation increase in SIA, translating to approximately 48 additional vote coordinations per Congress). Crucially, this effect is more than twice as large between members of different parties, while it is marginal and unreliable among members of the same party. Subsequent analysis shows that the bipartisan coordination effect of SIA is particularly activated under the presence of party pressure, suggesting that SIA functions to counterbalance party pressure. These coordination effects are estimated using a doubly robust approach and dynamic identification, and inferred while modeling the error structure of dyadic data. This study reveals that floor speeches are not merely instruments for individual or partisan branding, but serve as effective informal channels for bipartisan coordination, highlighting the deliberative potential of Congress.
