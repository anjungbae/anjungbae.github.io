Working Papers

"Contingent Congress: Shared Issue Attention in the U.S. House of Representatives, 1873-2010."
Abstract: Are legislators’ remarks merely a branding strategy or communicative acts? While cross-cutting communication among members can facilitate informed and legitimate policy-making, an increasingly partisan environment may undermine the deliberative capacity of the first branch. We use members’ floor speeches in the U.S. House of Representatives from 1873 to 2010 to examine how members share policy attention and whether that attention is shaped through communicative processes. The structure of the congressional shared issue attention reveals dense signals across party lines, despite a recent slight increase in partisan sorting. We then analyze whether a member’s speech influences another’s. The identified causal speech diffusion appears widely across the period. Importantly, cross-party diffusion tracks within-party diffusion across Congresses, suggesting communicative influence not bounded by party boundaries. Such cross-party diffusion is not reduced to the majority’s unilateral control.

"Informal Mediation: Shared Issue Attention and Bipartisan Coordination in the U.S. House of Representatives, 1873-2010."
Abstract: For congressional mediation to function effectively, members must engage in the exercise of social influence capable of fostering behavioral coordination, such as voting agreement. This paper examines the informal coordination that members engage in through public speeches during the period from 1873 to 2010. Exploiting the temporal variation of Shared Issue Attention (SIA) reflected in topics and frames of members' speeches, we assess its coordination effects on members' voting agreement within- and across the parties. We find that a higher SIA exerts a robust positive effect on legislative voting agreement. Specifically, this effect is more than twice as large among members across the parties (yielding an 8.3 percentage-point increase per one standard deviation increase in SIA; approximately 97 extra cross-party votes per Congress), whereas it is negligible and unreliable among members from the same party. Subsequent analysis reveals that the cross-partisan cooperative effect of SIA is particularly activated in the presence of party pressure, suggesting that SIA serves to counterbalance it. These findings highlight the deliberative capacity of Congress --- members' ability to search coordination points through collectively shaping legislative discourse --- particularly when they are subject to pressures for party cohesion.

"Extremists, Entertainers, or Experts? A Field Experiment on Congressional Incentives for Non-Electoral Representation." with William Minozzi & Michael A. Neblo
Abstract: The nationalization of politics gives representatives the motive to reach beyond their electoral constituencies. While non-electoral representation can serve some democratic purposes, competition over informal constituencies encourages representatives to build personal brands rather than develop policy knowledge. To assess whether democratic innovations can counterbalance these incentives, we test whether citizens are receptive to substantive persuasion. We do so by convening a Deliberative Town Hall that pairs a national sample of Americans with congressional committee leaders to discuss policies in their portfolios. We find that committee leaders can shift opinion, especially on “hard sell” issues, where bipartisan mass opinion initially skews against elite consensus. Persuasion was strongest among participants least likely to engage with standard politics. These findings suggest that representatives can lead opinion beyond their districts without relying solely on brands, party cues, or identity affiliations, and that doing so is especially effective with otherwise disaffected citizens.

"Committee Expertise Formation." (Forthcoming book chapter)
Abstract: Congressional committees are theoretically posited as engines of policy expertise, yet the micro-foundations of how this expertise emerges from member interactions remain under-explored. This study investigates the formation of collective expertise by analyzing the causal diffusion of linguistic patterns within U.S. House select committee hearings. Leveraging natural language processing and a causal network design, I examine influence across semantic, topical, and moral dimensions of speech. Contrary to models emphasizing strict hierarchy or partisan segmentation, the analysis reveals that discursive influence is strikingly reciprocal and horizontal. These findings challenge static power-based views, identifying committees as genuine deliberative spaces where substantive exchange drives the collective definition of policy reality.
